Basic, Single-Clause Sentences

In this section, we want to handle transitive, intransitive, and copular sentences in their basic word order (i.e. not changed by focus, topic, interrogative, or negation). The data is in surface form, so the grammar must model surface structure, not the underlying structure before any derivational movement.

The trees you see will be in an X-bar theory form, as much as is possible for modelling the surface structure without movement. This means that sentences are Inflectional Phrases (IPs) rather than the traditional S. The subject usually occupies the specifier position, the head is an inflectional or auxiliary element, and the complement is the VP predicate. For example, the tree for a transitive sentence in an SVO language, like English will be:
              IP
      ________|_________
     DP                I'
     |           ______|_______
  subject       Aux           VP 
                         _____|______
                        V           DP
                        |           |
                       verb       object

SOV, VOS, and OVS word orders can be handled by taking the mirror image of the appropriate subtrees. VSO and OSV word order must have the subject internal to the VP, however, so the flatter structure shown here for VSO word order will be used:
         IP
         |
         I'
   ______|_______
  Aux           VP
        ________|________
        V       DP      DP
        |       |       |
       verb  subject  object
