Nominal Phrases - Possessors

The next type of modifier to consider is possessors. Possession can normally be expressed by a possessive pronoun (to be addressed in section 6 Pronouns and Proper Names) or by a full nominal phrase. Many languages also add some kind of marking, such as the 's in English, as an indication of possession. These markings are either affixes (or clitics) on the head noun or phrase-level clitics which attach to one end of the whole phrase. The English marking is this second type, because alongside phrases where the 's appears to attach to the head noun, as in the boy's mother, there are phrases which clearly show that the clitic attaches to the end of the whole nominal phrase, such as the boy that I just talked to's mother or the girl in green's wonderful speech.


Think about how possession is expressed in your language. We won't be able to handle pre/post-positional phrases until section 7 Pre/Post-positional Phrases and relative clauses within nominal phrases until section 15 Fully Modified Nominal Phrases, but examples such as the English ones above will clarify for you what type of possessive marking you have.

Note also that possessors can be embedded in one another, as in the boy's sister's dog. Include such an example in your test data.
