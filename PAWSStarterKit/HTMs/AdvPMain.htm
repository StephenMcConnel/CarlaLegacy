Adverbs and AdvP

Adverbs express four basic types of information:

  • time,
  • location,
  • manner,
  • and reason.
These types have different characteristics as to which positions they may occupy and how they may be modified, so they need to be distinguished from each other by a feature. We'll deal with each in turn.


Temporal adverbs include the English words: already, then, soon, now, later, and always. Think of similar words in your language. These need to be distinguished from other types of words or phrases that can also express the time in a sentence, such as: nominal phrases yesterday, last night, next year, tomorrow, a long time ago; prepositional phrases for a long time, in a little while, at 10 oclock'; and adverbial clauses while you are working, when Sue arrived.


   Example English temporal adverbial phrases:
1. very soon.
2. much later.
3. only now.

Think of examples of temporal adverbs and include any degree words which can modify them.

Key your examples here:

In the lexicon, key the feature temporal on all adverbs that express time. You can also add this feature to nouns such as night, year, day, yesterday, tomorrow, time, while (the noun, we'll deal later with the complementizer for an adverbial clause), and o'clock.
